  <p><strong>The Physics of Natural Latex</strong></p>

  <p><strong>Dunlop vs. Talalay</strong></p>
  <p>
    - Dunlop: Traditional process. Denser at the base, slightly firmer overall. High durability and structural support. Best suited for support/base layers.<br>
    - Talalay: Vacuum and flash-freezing process creates a more uniform open-cell structure. Softer, more elastic, and more breathable. Best suited for comfort layers.
  </p>

  <p><strong>ILD (Indentation Load Deflection)</strong><br>
  Measure of firmness defined as the force (lbs) required to compress a 4"x4"x4" latex sample by 25%.</p>

  <p>
    - Soft: 19–21 ILD<br>
    - Medium: 24–26 ILD<br>
    - Firm: 28–32 ILD
  </p>

  <p><strong>cphi-Sleep Configuration</strong></p>
  <p>
    3" Firm Dunlop (28–32 ILD) base layer for structural support + 2" Soft/Medium Talalay (19–24 ILD) comfort layer.<br>
    This configuration produces responsive “floating” support rather than the slow viscoelastic sink associated with memory foam systems.
  </p>

  <p><strong>Thermal Dynamics</strong></p>
  <p>
    Natural latex has an open-cell structure that promotes continuous airflow and passive heat dissipation.<br>
    Polyurethane foams are predominantly closed-cell, which traps heat and moisture.<br>
    No gel additives are required—cooling behavior is intrinsic to the material structure.
  </p>

  <p><strong>Durability</strong></p>
  <p>
    Natural latex retains elasticity and structural resilience for approximately 15–20+ years under normal use conditions.<br>
    Conventional polyurethane foam systems typically degrade within 5–8 years, losing support and shedding particulate breakdown products.
  </p>
